In this video, we will focus on hydrocarbons. A hydrocarbon is a compound that contains only carbon and hydrogen atoms. Methane (CH₄) is a hydrocarbon, as is ethane (C₂H₆).

Hydrocarbons can be broadly categorized into two main types: aliphatic hydrocarbons and aromatic hydrocarbons. Aliphatic hydrocarbons include molecules such as alkanes, alkenes, and alkynes. Alkenes possess carbon-carbon double bonds, while alkynes contain carbon-carbon triple bonds. Aromatic hydrocarbons, on the other hand, are characterized by a special type of ring structure, the most popular example being the benzene ring, which has three alternating double bonds within a six-membered carbon ring. Aromatic hydrocarbons are also known as arenes.

It is also important to distinguish between saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbons. Saturated hydrocarbons are alkanes. They are "saturated" because they are completely filled with hydrogen atoms, meaning they have the maximum number of hydrogen atoms possible per carbon atom. Alkanes contain only single bonds (sigma bonds) between carbon atoms and are therefore saturated.

Unsaturated hydrocarbons, however, contain at least one pi bond, which means they have carbon-carbon double bonds, triple bonds, or aromatic rings. Alkenes, with their carbon-carbon double bonds, are unsaturated. Alkynes, with their carbon-carbon triple bonds, are also unsaturated. Benzene rings, being aromatic, are another example of unsaturated hydrocarbons. Thus, if a molecule contains any double or triple bonds, it is an unsaturated hydrocarbon; if it contains only single bonds, it is a saturated hydrocarbon. To reiterate, unsaturated hydrocarbons include alkenes, alkynes, and aromatic rings, while only alkanes are saturated.

Let's discuss alkanes, the saturated hydrocarbons. The general formula for an alkane is CnH2n+2.
The first few alkanes are:
*   CH₄: Methane
*   C₂H₆: Ethane
*   C₃H₈: Propane
*   C₄H₁₀: Butane
*   C₅H₁₂: Pentane
*   C₆H₁₄: Hexane
*   C₇H₁₆: Heptane
*   C₈H₁₈: Octane
*   C₉H₂₀: Nonane
*   C₁₀H₂₂: Decane

*   C₁₅H₃₂: Pentadecane (penta- for five, deca- for ten, 5+10=15)

*   C₂₀H₄₂: Icosane

Next, let's consider alkenes. If an alkene contains only one double bond (one pi bond), its general formula is CnH2n. For each additional double bond present in the alkene, the number of hydrogen atoms decreases by two. For example, if an alkene has two pi bonds, its formula would be CnH2n-2.

Consider 2-butene, which has four carbon atoms. Its structure can be drawn as:
CH₃-CH=CH-CH₃
Counting the atoms, it has C₄H₈. Here, n=4, and 2n = 2 × 4 = 8, which fits the CnH2n formula. This specific example shows trans-2-butene, where the two methyl groups are on opposite sides of the double bond. If it were cis-2-butene, the methyl groups would be on the same side.

It is interesting to note that cycloalkanes, which are alkanes in a ring structure, also share the general formula CnH2n if they contain one ring. If a cycloalkane has two rings, its formula becomes CnH2n-2. For example, cyclopentane has the formula C₅H₁₀. In cyclopentane, each carbon atom is bonded to two other carbon atoms and two hydrogen atoms, resulting in a total of five carbon atoms and ten hydrogen atoms.

Let's compare this to a typical alkane like pentane, which has the formula C₅H₁₂ based on CnH2n+2. Alkanes have the maximum number of hydrogens per carbon atom and are therefore saturated. Anytime a ring or a double bond is introduced into a molecule, two hydrogen atoms are lost. This is why alkenes and cycloalkanes are unsaturated; they do not have the maximum number of hydrogen atoms they could potentially have.

If a triple bond is introduced, four hydrogen atoms are lost. Alkynes, which contain a triple bond, have the chemical formula CnH2n-2, assuming there is one triple bond. For every triple bond, four hydrogen atoms are lost compared to the corresponding alkane.

For example, acetylene (ethyne) is C₂H₂. If we compare it to ethane (C₂H₆), which has the same number of carbons, we see a difference of four hydrogen atoms (6 - 2 = 4).

Another example is 2-butyne, which has the structure:
CH₃-C≡C-CH₃
This molecule is C₄H₆. Comparing it to butane (C₄H₁₀), we again observe a difference of four hydrogen atoms (10 - 6 = 4).

In summary, introducing a double bond or a ring results in the loss of two hydrogen atoms. Introducing a triple bond results in the loss of four hydrogen atoms.

Hydrocarbons are fundamental molecules in organic chemistry, defined simply as compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen atoms.
